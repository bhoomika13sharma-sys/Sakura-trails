✦ When to Visit ✦

Japan Through the Seasons

Every season reveals a different Japan — each with its own palette, festivals, and unmissable experiences for the travelling soul.

🌸

Spring

March — May

Cherry blossoms blanket every city in pink. Hanami picnics, warm days, and Japan's most photographed season. Book 6 months ahead.

Peak Season
🌊

Summer

June — August

Vibrant matsuri festivals, fireworks, and lush greenery. Hot and humid, but the Obon festival and mountain escapes make it magical.

Festival Season
🍁

Autumn

September — November

Crimson maple leaves — koyo — transform Japan into a fiery canvas. Perfect cool weather, fewer crowds, and some of the best food of the year.

Best for Photos
❄️

Winter

December — February

Snow-dusted temples, steaming onsen hot springs, and the Sapporo Snow Festival. The quietest season with dramatic, haiku-worthy landscapes.

Budget Friendly

✦ Japanese Cuisine ✦

Taste Japan

Japan's culinary culture is a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. Each city has its own dish, each dish its own obsessive perfection.

🍣
📌 Tokyo

Edomae Sushi

Tokyo-style sushi — fresh nigiri pressed by hand atop seasoned rice, served at intimate counters where chefs train for a decade.

🍜
📌 Tokyo / Osaka

Tonkotsu Ramen

Rich pork bone broth simmered 12 hours, silky noodles, soft chashu pork, and a perfectly marinated soft-boiled egg. A bowl of pure comfort.

🍤
📌 Kyoto

Kaiseki Cuisine

Japan's ultimate fine dining — a multi-course seasonal feast of exquisite small dishes, each a meditation on texture, colour, and flavour.

🐙
📌 Osaka

Takoyaki

Osaka's beloved octopus balls — golden, crispy outside, molten inside — topped with bonito flakes, mayo, and sweet-savoury sauce. Eaten straight from the pan.

🍵
📌 Kyoto / Nara

Matcha Everything

Ceremonial grade green tea whisked frothy in lacquered bowls, or transformed into ice cream, mochi, cakes, and lattes across Kyoto's teahouses.

🥩
📌 Kobe / Kyoto

Wagyu Beef

Marble-patterned Kobe beef so tender it dissolves. Grilled simply at tableside, or served as sukiyaki — one of the great luxury food experiences on earth.

✦ Insider Advice ✦

Essential Travel Tips

From a veteran Japan traveller — the practical details that make the difference between a good trip and an unforgettable one.

🚅

Get a JR Pass

A Japan Rail Pass covers Shinkansen bullet trains, local JR lines, and some ferries. Buy before you leave home — it's significantly cheaper and saves time at the station. The 7-day pass is ideal for 2–3 cities.

Transport
💳

IC Card is Essential

Load a Suica or PASMO IC card at any JR machine for seamless travel on trains, buses, and even convenience stores. One card works across every city — the single most practical item you'll carry throughout Japan.

Transport
📅

Book Sakura Season Early

Cherry blossom season (late March–mid April) is Japan's most magical time. Hotels and flights sell out months in advance. Book at least 5–6 months ahead. Early morning visits to parks beat the crowds beautifully.

Timing
🍱

Eat at Convenience Stores

Japan's 7-Eleven, Lawson, and FamilyMart are a revelation — fresh onigiri, proper hot meals, coffee, and incredible pastries available 24 hours. Locals eat here daily and proudly. Never skip them.

Food
👟

Wear Slip-On Shoes

Temples, traditional restaurants, and ryokan require removing shoes frequently. Slip-ons save precious seconds and spare you the balancing performance of unlacing shoes on a narrow wooden step in public.

Culture
📱

Download Offline Maps

Rent pocket Wi-Fi at the airport or buy a data SIM. Download Google Maps offline for every city. Japan's train network is extraordinary but complex — having navigation at all times is genuinely essential.

Planning
🏨

Stay in a Ryokan Once

A traditional ryokan — tatami floors, futon beds, yukata robes, onsen baths, and a multi-course kaiseki dinner — is one of Japan's greatest experiences. Budget ¥15,000–¥30,000 per person per night for a real one.

Accommodation
🙇

Respect Temple Etiquette

Bow slightly when entering shrines. Cleanse hands at the temizuya water pavilion. Speak softly. Don't photograph people praying. Remove shoes when required. Japanese visitors are respectful — follow their lead naturally.

Culture
💴

Carry Cash Always

Japan is still largely cash-based outside tourist areas. Smaller restaurants, market stalls, and temple entry counters often don't accept cards. 7-Eleven ATMs accept international cards reliably — always useful to know.
